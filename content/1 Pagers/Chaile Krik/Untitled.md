# The Diagnosis of a Nation

### **Section 1.1: The Naïve Conviction**

It began with a simple conviction: maybe, just maybe, a better argument could heal a divided world. In the wake of the 2020 election and the deepening cultural cold war, the battle looked like one of information. Each side lived in its own algorithm, fed a diet of outrage and validation, until two parallel realities emerged that could no longer talk to each other. The air was thick with manipulation, fallacy, and bad faith.

My impulse wasn't to join the fight, but to rise above it. I didn't want to be a soldier-I wanted to be an architect. I wanted to build a bridge of reason strong enough to span the chasm. If people could be shown the flaws in their thinking-the hidden persuaders, the emotional triggers, the untested assumptions-then surely the fever would break. A person freed from their own mental prison could not be so easily led into conflict.

This was the hope: that logic could be a lifeline. That by offering tools for clear thinking, we might return to a shared reality.

---

### **Section 1.1A: The Core Diagnosis**

But the more I studied, the clearer it became: the problem wasn't just misinformation. It was belief itself.

Most people have no idea how their beliefs are formed. They absorb them-from parents, from childhood impressions, from the algorithm-until those beliefs feel less like ideas and more like limbs. Touch them, and it doesn't feel like debate; it feels like amputation. To press against a belief is not to challenge someone's logic, but to challenge their very self. No wonder the reaction is always defensive, always patterned, always fierce.

That realization forced me into painful self-interrogation. _Why am I doing this? What do I really want? What's my actual goal?_

The answer wasn't conversion. I wasn't trying to make people think like me-that would make me the very ideologue I was resisting. The real goal was far simpler, and far harder: to get people-myself first-to actually examine their own beliefs. To run a diagnostic on the code driving their lives. To arrive at honest conclusions, even if they landed right back where they started.


---

## **Section 1.2: The Personal Toolkit**

To do this, I began forging a set of questions. A protocol. Not a weapon to use on others, but a mirror for myself. It had to be non-intrusive, elegant, and based on first principles. The questions were simple, but their implications were deep:

- **Who benefits if I believe this?** (A question of Power)
- **What emotion is this belief activating in me?** (A question of Feeling)
- **Did I consciously choose this, or did repetition program it into me?** (A question of Origin)

This was the toolkit. A way to deconstruct belief, get to the studs, and see if the foundation was solid. On paper, it was a perfect, logical machine.


---

## **Section 1.3: The Failure in the Mind's Eye**

And here is the truth: even as I refined it, I knew it would fail.

I kept running a simulation in my head. I'd picture myself on a stage, a Charlie Kirk figure, laying out this elegant, rational system to a crowd. And in this mental theater, the reaction was always the same: a mix of confusion, anger, and indifference. It wouldn't resonate. It couldn't.

Why? Because I was bringing a calculator to a fistfight.

The very premise was flawed. I was trying to use a logical tool to solve a problem that was never rooted in logic. People's beliefs are held in place by emotion, by tribal identity, by a deep-seated need for belonging and meaning. My protocol, as logical as it was, would bounce right off that emotional armor. Worse, by treating their identity-level belief as a mere intellectual puzzle to be solved, it was fundamentally disrespectful. It was the antithesis of the American ideal I was trying to save-the harmonious coexistence of different peoples.

## **Section 1.4: The Unavoidable Conclusion**

When I first began to build the logical framework, I tested it on myself. I didn't want to arm others with something I hadn't already run through my own filters. So I steel-manned my arguments, pushing them as hard as I could.

My beliefs, I told myself, are rooted in history, in the traditions of this nation. I believe they are right. Yet as I pressed deeper, I uncovered a disquieting symmetry. For every argument I could construct on my side, I could imagine an equal argument-equally weighted by emotion, identity, and tribal loyalty-on the other.

That discovery shifted everything. It showed me that no matter how elegantly someone tried to deconstruct my convictions from the outside, it would not work on me. Why? Because my beliefs are not just conclusions; they are part of who I am. They are woven into my identity. And if logic and evidence alone couldn't shake me, why would I believe they could shake anyone else?

It was in that moment I felt the first check in my spirit. I asked a friend: _"If I were to walk into a deeply entrenched group-any group-with a simple, common-sense idea, stripped of politics or religion, do you think they would listen?"_

They didn't pause. They just said, _"No."_

That "no" landed harder than any counterargument. It wasn't just opinion-it was diagnosis. In that instant, the bridge I thought I was building collapsed into the very chasm I was trying to span.

And yet, what looked like collapse was actually clarity. The problem wasn't that I had been wrong to care, or wrong to build. The problem was the bias I had been carrying: the humanistic belief that my logic, my evidence, and my passion could overturn a heart that had willingly chosen its ground. That was the illusion.

The shedding of that bias was liberation. It freed me from carrying a burden that was never mine. The Holy Spirit does the work of transformation; my role is not to coerce or convince, but to witness and to live the truth. My failure wasn't proof of delusion. It was proof of the boundary line between what belongs to me and what belongs to God.



### **Who I am : A Proclamation of The Cleaving**


"I used to believe we were all on the same team. Americans. A little different, sure, but at our core, the same. I believed that with enough evidence, enough goodwill, enough patient explanation, we could bridge any divide. I built whole frameworks around that idea.

And I'll be honest with you-I was wrong.

Not wrong because the logic was bad or the evidence weak. Wrong because I put more faith in my own bridge-building than I did in God's eternal purpose. I thought my enemy was bad ideas. But what I've come to realize is that this isn't about Republican or Democrat, Left or Right. That's the theater. The real conflict is the oldest one: good versus evil. Truth versus the lie.

And in that conflict, there is no neutral ground. To not choose is to drift into whatever side happens to be winning the cultural narrative. And let's not fool ourselves-that side is not aligned with Logos, with Christ, with truth.

For a long time that crushed me. I believe in second chances. It's the American way. It's the Christian way. But what do you do when a man is falling and calls it flying? When he won't even admit he's fallen? That's where my sadness set in.

So I started looking for language. I wanted a word that wasn't "war." Because war is too physical-bodies, borders, resources. This is something else. This is fought over the very operating system of reality: truth, life, man, woman, freedom, God Himself.

I needed a word that meant:

- A fundamental, irreconcilable divide.
    
- A total commitment to one side.
    
- A conflict where neutrality is impossible.
    
- A struggle whose stakes are eternal.
    
- An action that is firm and decisive, but not necessarily violent.
    

I found it.

**Good: The Contention.** Strong, but too intellectual.  
**Better: The Schism.** Final, but still not complete.  
**The Perfect Word: The Cleaving.**

Because to cleave means two things at once:

1. To split, to sever, to divide.
    
2. To cling, to adhere, to hold fast.
    

That is it. That is the ethos.

The rules of The Cleaving are simple:

1. **Adhere, don't attack.** My job is to stand coherent in truth, not to hammer others into it.
    
2. **The line draws itself.** If I live in the light, the contrast is obvious. I don't have to carve the sand. I _am_ the line.
    
3. **Victory is faithfulness, not conversion.** If one soul crosses the chasm because they saw me hold fast, praise God. But the measure is obedience, not numbers.
    
4. **Build, don't demolish.** The world is already tearing itself down. My task is to construct the true, the good, the beautiful-a city of light, not rubble.
    

And so here is my proclamation:

From this day forward, my mission is **To Cleave.**

I will cleave _to_ my God, my family, the eternal truths of Scripture, the liberty endowed by our Creator, and the coherent reality He has designed.  
And in doing so, I will cleave _from_ a world that calls good evil and evil good. I will not argue with it. I will not persuade it. I will not build bridges to it. I will build a fortress of truth, a city of light, and let the line reveal itself.

My posture is building, not battle. But do not mistake this for weakness. The Tree of Liberty, as Jefferson said, must sometimes be refreshed with the blood of patriots and tyrants. My cleaving to the truth also means cleaving to the God-given right to defend my family, my faith, and my freedom.

**Don't tread on me.**

There are two paths. The choice is yours. There is no middle ground."


---
### **The Core Argument: Rewritten & Refined**

Some might find it audacious, but I believe the words of Tucker Carlson are a startlingly accurate diagnosis of our time: this is not about Left vs. Right, Democrat vs. Republican. That is the theater. The real, underlying conflict is the ancient one: the war between good and evil.

If I'm going to make a claim that foundational, I must anchor it not in the shifting sands of politics or culture, but on solid rock. There is only one standard that has never changed from the beginning of time until now: Jesus Christ. If you filter right and wrong through the Bible, the line holds, because His definition of good has never wavered.

And that standard is higher and more personal than any political ideology. Jesus makes it clear:

- _"You have heard that it was said, 'You shall not commit adultery.' But I tell you that anyone who looks at a woman lustfully has already committed adultery with her in his heart."_
- _"You have heard that it was said... 'You shall not murder.' But I tell you that anyone who is angry with a brother or sister will be subject to judgment."_
- _"You have heard that it was said, 'Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.' But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you."_

This is the standard that cuts deeper than any headline. It doesn't just expose the flaws in "them"; it relentlessly exposes the flaws in "me."

This is the true blueprint for **The Cleaving**. It isn't my idea or a new political posture. It is the very life Jesus modeled. On one hand, He was a relentless force against corrupt systems-overturning the tables in the Temple, calling the Pharisees "whitewashed tombs," refusing the political traps of Rome. That is **cleaving from**: a clean, decisive separation from falsehood.

Yet at the same time, He was radically personal and scandalously compassionate. He ate with tax collectors, touched lepers, and offered forgiveness to the woman caught in sin. That is **cleaving to**: an unbreakable adherence to God's love for the individual.

He proved that truth and love are not in opposition. True Cleaving demands both: a total break from the world's lies, and a total, compassionate adherence to God's design.

And that is where The Cleaving must begin for us. Not with the righteous claim that our tribe is correct, but with the humble admission that we all fall short of His standard and desperately need to cling to the One who is.

Because Jesus Himself is the line. He is the Logos, the perfect Truth made flesh. And the only test that matters is not which flag we fly or which party we vote for, but whether our actions, our loves, and our loyalties align with Him.

That is the story. That is the standard. That is the call.
