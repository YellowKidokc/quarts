# Locating Quantum Physics Experimental Measurement Data (1940-1955) from Bell Labs, MIT, Princeton, and NIST: An Archival Investigation with a Focus on May 1948

## I. Overview of Archival Resources for Quantum Physics Experimental Data (1940-1955)

The period between 1940 and 1955 represents a transformative era in physics, witnessing the practical applications of quantum mechanics burgeon, particularly in the United States. Research at major laboratories such as Bell Telephone Laboratories, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), Princeton University, and the National Bureau of Standards (NBS, later NIST) was pivotal. Locating comprehensive experimental measurement data from this period requires a systematic approach to their respective archival resources, primarily published journals and internal report series.

### A. Bell Laboratories: Key Holdings and Access Points

Bell Telephone Laboratories (Bell Labs) was a powerhouse of innovation during this period, with its research output extensively documented in its flagship publication and, presumably, in internal records.

**Primary Published Source: Bell Labs Technical Journal (BLTJ)** The _Bell Labs Technical Journal_, known as the _Bell System Technical Journal_ (BSTJ) during the 1940-1955 timeframe, stands as the most critical published repository of Bell Labs' scientific and engineering achievements. This peer-reviewed journal disseminated articles from Bell Labs researchers across a wide spectrum of disciplines.  

Access to this invaluable resource is multifaceted. Nokia, the current parent company of Bell Labs, has partnered with the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) to provide comprehensive digital access to the entire BLTJ archive through the IEEE Xplore digital library. This digital pathway is indispensable for any systematic review of published experimental data. Additionally, the Internet Archive hosts volumes of the BSTJ spanning 1922 to 1957 , and Worldradiohistory.com provides access to issues from 1922 to 1960 , offering alternative avenues for accessing this material.  

The content of the BLTJ, while broadly focused on information and communications technology, contains seminal works of direct relevance to quantum physics. Beyond its foundational papers in information theory by Claude Shannon (e.g., his 1948 work ), mathematics, and data transmission, the journal published groundbreaking research in semiconductor physics. Notably, articles by John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley in 1949 detailed the physics of transistor action, which is fundamentally rooted in quantum mechanics. These papers are primary sources for experimental data in solid-state physics, a key branch of quantum physics during the period.  

It is important to note the journal's publication history for accurate temporal analysis. The BSTJ was published quarterly from 1922 to 1951. Despite name changes over its history, the journal maintained its volume sequencing, ensuring a degree of continuity for researchers.  

The significance of the BLTJ articles on transistor action by Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley extends beyond mere engineering. These publications represent crucial experimental data from the forefront of semiconductor physics. The "physical principles" elucidated directly involve quantum phenomena within solid materials. Solid-state physics, particularly the study of semiconductors, emerged as a dominant research area in the post-World War II era, and its theoretical underpinnings are deeply quantum mechanical. Bell Labs was a leading institution in this domain, culminating in the invention of the transistor. Consequently, these 1949 BLTJ articles are primary sources for quantum-related experimental measurement data from Bell Labs within the specified 1940-1955 period. Any investigation into anomalies around May 1948 must consider the experimental trajectory that led to these landmark publications.  

**Internal Reports and Lab Notebooks (Potential)** While the provided documentation does not explicitly detail digitized quantum physics lab notebooks from Bell Labs for this period (Alexander Graham Bell's earlier, unrelated notebooks are mentioned ), the intensive experimental work underpinning the transistor's development implies a vast repository of internal experimental data and detailed record-keeping. The existence of "Archives Records Management Services at AT&T Bell Laboratories" suggests a formal archival function was in place, which may hold such records.  

Considering the May 1948 focus, it is crucial to recognize the timeline of transistor development. The transistor was publicly announced in June 1948, with pivotal experiments occurring in December 1947. The detailed scientific papers by Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley appeared in the BLTJ in 1949. Therefore, May 1948 falls within a period of intense, ongoing experimentation and data analysis  

_before_ comprehensive publication. Any measurement anomalies or significant precision changes in quantum-related experimental data at Bell Labs around May 1948 would most likely be documented in internal laboratory notebooks or progress reports associated with the semiconductor research program. While Shannon's 1948 papers are historically significant , they pertain to information theory and are not direct sources of experimental quantum physics measurements. The BLTJ issues from around May 1948 (e.g., Volume 27 ) might contain other physics research, but the most fertile ground for novel quantum measurement data and potential anomalies was undoubtedly the semiconductor program.  

### B. Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT): RLE Archives and Distinctive Collections

MIT's contributions to physics during this era were substantial, largely channeled through its specialized research laboratories and documented in their archival collections.

**Primary Archival Unit: MIT Libraries Department of Distinctive Collections** The Department of Distinctive Collections at MIT Libraries is the central repository for historical scientific records, including those from the MIT Center for Theoretical Physics (CTP) and, critically for this query, the Research Laboratory of Electronics (RLE). Materials within these collections are generally open for research use, although specific conditions, particularly regarding publication rights, may apply and require consultation with the archive.  

**Key Resource: Research Laboratory of Electronics (RLE)** The RLE, founded on January 1, 1946, was the direct successor to the renowned MIT Radiation Laboratory ("RadLab") of World War II. The RadLab itself, operational from 1940 to 1945, was primarily focused on the development of microwave radar systems. Upon its establishment, RLE inherited significant expertise and equipment from RadLab, redirecting these resources towards basic research. Early RLE research encompassed physical electronics (including cathodes, electronic emission, and gaseous conduction), microwave physics, the electromagnetic properties of matter, and microwave communication principles. These areas are foundational to understanding quantum phenomena, particularly in the realms of physical electronics and the interaction of microwaves with matter.  

**RLE Progress Reports and Technical Reports** are paramount for unearthing experimental data. Digital versions of some RLE Progress Reports are accessible via DSpace@MIT. Specifically, RLE Progress Report No. 007, dated October 15, 1947, and RLE Progress Report No. 009, dated April 15, 1948, have been identified. These reports are invaluable as they list ongoing research projects, publications by RLE members, and often summarize key findings. For instance, Report No. 007 mentions a section on "Modern Electronic Techniques Applied to Physics and Engineering" , while Report No. 009 details work in "Communications Research," "Traveling-Wave Tubes," and "Magnetron Development". These reports are prime targets for identifying specific experiments and potentially, associated data or references to more detailed internal documents. Accessing the full content of these and other reports from the 1947-1949 period is essential, as initial searches indicated that specific publication details require browsing these historical documents.  

The transition of RLE from the mission-driven RadLab to a basic research center in 1946 created a fertile environment for novel experimental work. The advanced microwave technology and expertise honed for radar development were now available for fundamental investigations into the quantum nature of matter and radiation. This scenario often leads to the application of established, high-precision techniques to new scientific questions, potentially revealing previously unobserved phenomena or enabling measurements of unprecedented accuracy. RLE's early investigations into "physical electronics" and "microwave physics" would have directly involved experiments designed to test quantum theories. Thus, the RLE Progress Reports serve as crucial documentary evidence of this pivotal period.  

**Lab Notebooks** While MIT currently provides modern electronic lab notebook solutions like LabArchives , access to historical, physical lab notebooks from the 1940-1955 period would necessitate direct inquiry with the Department of Distinctive Collections. Available online materials, such as lab guides for more recent physics experiments , do not cover the historical notebooks relevant to this query.  

For the specific May 1948 timeframe, RLE Progress Report No. 009, dated April 15, 1948 , is a key document. This report, along with any internal laboratory data, memoranda, or subsequent progress reports generated in the weeks immediately following its release, would be the most probable sources for documented anomalies or changes in measurement precision around May 1948 at MIT. The research areas listed within Report No. 009, such as "Traveling-Wave Tubes" and "Magnetron Development," involve sophisticated electron physics and microwave interactions. These are fields where subtle quantum effects or unexpected behaviors might manifest, especially as experimental precision improved in the post-war scientific landscape.  

### C. Princeton University: Physics Department Records and Special Collections

Princeton University's Physics Department, with its strong theoretical underpinnings and experimental facilities, played a significant role in the development of modern physics.

**Primary Archival Unit: Princeton University Library Special Collections, including Seeley G. Mudd Manuscript Library** The Special Collections at Princeton University Library, particularly the Seeley G. Mudd Manuscript Library, are the custodians of the Physics Department Records (collection identifier AC133), which span from 1909 to 2015. These collections are generally accessible to the public for research. However, a notable constraint for immediate remote access to undigitized materials is the current pause on digitization requests from non-Princeton researchers, effective until July 1, 2025.  

**Key Resource: Physics Department Records (AC133)** This collection is organized into several series, some of which are highly pertinent to this investigation:

- **Series 1: Chairman H. D. Smyth Records (1933-1953):** This series is of exceptional relevance as it covers the departmental leadership throughout the 1940-1955 period. It includes files on "Departmental Business," "Research Committee" activities, and correspondence with prominent physicists of the era. Such records could illuminate the direction of experimental programs and discussions of results.  
    
- **Series 3: Technical Reports (1943-1962):** This series is a prime candidate for containing raw or analyzed experimental data emanating from various physics research projects undertaken by the department.  
    
    The finding aid for AC133 also mentions records pertaining to an "Army Instruction Program, 1946-1950," which might include documentation of advanced physics experiments or associated data.  
    

Princeton's Physics Department was renowned for its strength in theoretical physics, with figures like Eugene Wigner and John von Neumann on its faculty, and close ties to Albert Einstein and Niels Bohr at the nearby Institute for Advanced Study (IAS). While the department also conducted experimental research, including nuclear physics utilizing its cyclotron (operational since the 1930s), this theoretical emphasis suggests that experimental data generated or sought by Princeton researchers might often have been closely linked to the verification or exploration of new theoretical predictions in quantum mechanics and nuclear physics. Archival searches within the Chairman's records, correspondence with theorists like Wigner, and Research Committee files should therefore look for discussions about experiments needed to test theories, interpretations of experimental findings (from Princeton or elsewhere), and data considered particularly significant for advancing theoretical understanding.  

**Digital Resources** Princeton's digital repositories, such as DataSpace and Digital PUL , primarily feature more recent dissertations, theses, and technical reports from the Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory (PPPL). Direct links to experimental quantum data from the 1940-1955 period are less evident in these digital platforms, aside from the finding aids that point to physical collections.  

Focusing on the May 1948 timeframe at Princeton, the most promising archival avenues are the "Chairman H. D. Smyth Records, 1933-1953" (specifically departmental business files, research committee minutes, and correspondence from 1948) and "Series 3, Technical Reports, 1943-1962". These collections could contain internal discussions or preliminary reports of ongoing experiments where anomalies or shifts in precision might have been first documented. An interesting entry in Smyth's records is "Ladenburg, R.: European Tour, 1948". Reports or correspondence arising from Rudolf Ladenburg's tour could offer valuable insights into the state of experimental physics in Europe at that time, including any discussions of anomalies or precision benchmarks, potentially contextualizing or influencing Princeton's own experimental efforts.  

### D. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST/NBS): Archival Data and Journal of Research

While the NIST campus in Gaithersburg, Maryland, is not generally open to the public, access to the archives for research purposes can be granted on a case-by-case basis. Inquiries and requests for assistance should be directed to museum@nist.gov or library@nist.gov.

Historical volumes are available through the NIST Digital Archives. Although a previous targeted search indicated potential difficulties in browsing specific years or topics directly within these digital archives , tables of contents for several relevant volumes are accessible: Volume 39 (1947) , Volume 40 (January-June 1948) , and Volume 41 (July-December 1948). These tables of contents are critical for identifying specific papers containing experimental data. The journal's scope encompasses metrology, physical science, engineering, applied mathematics, and statistics , making it highly relevant for research into precision measurements and the determination of atomic constants.  

NBS's core mission in metrology and the establishment of standards renders its publications and archival records primary sources for tracking the evolution of

For the 1940-1955 period, the "Historical Volumes in NIST Digital Archives" remains the key access point for published research.  

For investigating events around May 1948, the _Journal of Research of the NBS_ Volumes 40 (covering January-June 1948 ) and 41 (covering July-December 1948 ) are of paramount importance. ## II. Identifying Potential Quantum Physics Experimental Data Sets

The archival resources outlined provide avenues to diverse experimental data sets relevant to quantum physics. The nature of these data reflects the major scientific preoccupations and technological capabilities of the 1940-1955 period at each institution.

### A. Bell Labs: Focus on Early Semiconductor Physics, Microwave Studies, and Electron Dynamics.

Bell Labs' most prominent contribution to quantum-related experimental physics in this era was undoubtedly in **semiconductor research**. The invention of the point-contact transistor in December 1947 and the subsequent development of the junction transistor involved extensive experimentation. Data underpinning these breakthroughs would include measurements of conductivity, carrier mobility, current-voltage characteristics of p-n junctions, surface state effects, and amplification phenomena in germanium and, later, silicon. The 1949 BLTJ papers by Bardeen and Brattain ("Physical Principles Involved in Transistor Action") and Shockley ("The theory of p-n junctions in semiconductors and p-n junction transistors") are key published syntheses of this experimental work. The historic experiment of December 16, 1947, which first demonstrated voltage and power gain, is a critical data point ; subsequent experiments refining transistor performance and understanding its physics would yield further crucial datasets.  

The behavior observed by Bardeen and Brattain, which ultimately led to the transistor, was, from the perspective of then-current theories of semiconductor surface states, quite anomalous. Their experimental data, particularly concerning the behavior of point contacts on germanium and the influence of applied electric fields, represented a significant deviation from prior understanding. This "unexplained variation" was eventually resolved by Bardeen's new theoretical insights into surface physics. This sequence exemplifies how an apparent anomaly in experimental measurements can drive fundamental theoretical advancements. The early transistor experimental data, especially from late 1947 and early 1948, thus inherently contains results that were anomalous relative to the prevailing understanding, only to be explained by new quantum mechanical models of semiconductor surfaces.  

Beyond semiconductors, Bell Labs continued its strong tradition in **microwave research**. While much wartime effort had been directed towards radar systems, post-war investigations explored microwave components and phenomena with broader applications. Articles published in the BSTJ during 1948, such as W. R. Bennett's "Spectra of Quantized Signals," W. W. Mumford's "Maximally-Flat Filters in Wave Guide," and J. R. Pierce's "Transverse Fields in Traveling-Wave Tubes" , indicate ongoing experimental and theoretical activities with microwaves. Such research can be instrumental in probing quantum systems or developing tools for such probes.  

Additionally, fundamental studies of **electron emission and noise** were pursued. J. R. Pierce's 1948 BSTJ article "Noise in Resistances and Electron Streams" suggests experimental work on basic noise processes, many of which (like shot noise or thermal noise at low temperatures) have quantum origins or require quantum mechanics for a full description.  

### B. MIT: RLE Reports on Physical Electronics, Microwave Physics, Atomic/Nuclear Work.

The Research Laboratory of Electronics (RLE) at MIT, building on the legacy of the RadLab, focused on several areas rich in potential quantum physics data. In **physical electronics**, RLE continued investigations into cathodes, electronic emission phenomena, and gaseous conduction. Experiments in these domains could yield data on the quantum behavior of electrons in materials and in vacuum. The RLE Progress Report No. 7 (October 1947), specifically the section titled "Modern Electronic Techniques Applied to Physics and Engineering" by Polk, Galt, Maier, Demos, Mason, and others , is a critical starting point for identifying specific experiments and associated data from this period.  

RLE's profound expertise in microwaves, inherited from RadLab, was systematically applied to study the **electromagnetic properties of matter**. This line of inquiry naturally leads to microwave spectroscopy, a powerful tool for probing quantum states in atoms and molecules. The development of the first electronic analog correlator by Thomas Cheatham, Jr. in 1948 , and dedicated research on traveling-wave tubes and magnetrons detailed in RLE Progress Report No. 9 (April 1948) , involved precision microwave measurements and sophisticated electron beam physics, both of which could reveal quantum effects.  

The unique position of MIT's RLE, evolving from the engineering-focused RadLab, meant that cutting-edge advancements in microwave technology (such as improved magnetrons, sensitive detection systems, and signal processing techniques) were immediately available for application to fundamental physics questions. This synergy could have led to new types of quantum measurements or levels of precision not yet commonly achievable elsewhere. Microwave spectroscopy, for instance, became a cornerstone of quantum physics research, exemplified by the Lamb shift experiments at Columbia University and Charles Townes' subsequent work, both of whom had backgrounds in wartime radar research. RLE's investigations into the "electromagnetic properties of matter at microwave frequencies" and specific projects like "Traveling-Wave Tubes" and "Magnetron Development" would have involved experiments where quantum effects in materials or within electron beams could be precisely measured and documented. RLE reports are therefore key for finding data where engineering prowess intersected with fundamental quantum inquiries.  

While RLE's initial primary focus was on electronics and microwaves, its involvement in **atomic and nuclear physics** was emerging. By 1952, Jerrold Zacharias and his team had produced the first practical atomic clock based on atomic beam frequency standards , indicating a growing institutional capability and interest in precision atomic physics. Experimental work leading up to this achievement might well be present in RLE records from the 1940-1950 period. Furthermore, the Laboratory for Nuclear Science (LNS) was a sister laboratory to RLE , suggesting potential collaborations or sharing of advanced techniques relevant to nuclear and quantum physics.  

### C. Princeton: Records Related to Nuclear Physics, Cyclotron Experiments, Cosmic Rays, and Theoretical Support.

Princeton University's Physics Department, while housing a strong theoretical group, also maintained experimental programs, particularly in **nuclear physics**. The university operated a cyclotron from the 1930s , and experimental data derived from cyclotron-based research-such as particle scattering experiments, studies of nuclear reactions, and determinations of the properties of atomic nuclei-fall within the broad scope of quantum physics. Archival records related to the "Cyclotron, 1936-1937" may have successor files or related documentation extending into the 1940-1955 period, detailing ongoing experimental work. The post-war era was a period of significant development in nuclear models, such as the shell model and collective models. Princeton's experimental program, leveraging its cyclotron, was likely engaged in research aimed at testing these emerging theories. Data pertaining to nuclear energy levels, particle scattering cross-sections, and radioactive decay characteristics would be highly relevant and are likely to be found within the "Technical Reports" (Series 3 of AC133) and the research records of physicists involved in the nuclear program.  

Given Princeton's eminence in theoretical physics, with faculty like Eugene Wigner and John von Neumann, and its close association with figures like Albert Einstein and Niels Bohr at the Institute for Advanced Study , a significant portion of relevant material may lie in  

**theoretical work with strong experimental implications**. Correspondence and reports involving these individuals, as well as those of H.D. Smyth (the department chairman) , could contain discussions or analyses of experimental data-whether generated at Princeton or elsewhere-that were considered crucial for the development and validation of quantum theory. The files of specific physicists such as Rudolf Ladenburg, Allen Shenstone, Milton White, and John Wheeler are also important targets, as they may hold experimental data, analyses, or correspondence related to their research. The "Technical Reports" (Series 3 of collection AC133), covering 1943-1962 , remain prime candidates for containing raw or analyzed experimental data from a variety of physics research projects conducted at Princeton.  

### D. NIST/NBS: Precision Measurements, Spectroscopy, Atomic Constants, Radiation Physics.

The National Bureau of Standards was the nation's premier institution for **metrology and precision measurements**, making its records exceptionally valuable for this study. A core area was **atomic spectroscopy**. NBS served as a central hub for the collection, critical evaluation, and dissemination of atomic spectral data. The  

_Journal of Research of the NBS_ is replete with publications detailing precise wavelength measurements, determinations of atomic energy levels, and calculations or measurements of transition probabilities. Examples from the 1947-1948 period include Earle K. Plyler's extensive work on infrared absorption spectra of various molecules (e.g., carbon dioxide, carbon disulfide ) and U. Fano's theoretical contributions to understanding electric quadrupole coupling, which had direct implications for the interpretation of microwave spectroscopy experiments.  

NBS was also heavily involved in the **determination of fundamental constants**. Experiments aimed at establishing more precise values for constants such as the speed of light, the elementary charge, or Planck's constant are inherently relevant to quantum physics and often push the boundaries of measurement science.

Research in **radiation physics and X-ray standards** also features in NBS publications. An example is the 1948 paper by Wyckoff, Kennedy, and Bradford on the "Broad- and narrow-beam attenuation of 500- to 1,400-kilovolt x-rays in lead and concrete" , which details experimental work on the interaction of high-energy radiation with matter.  

While not always direct "quantum" experiments, precise measurements of **temperature standards and material properties** can reveal underlying quantum effects, especially in phenomena like specific heat at low temperatures or electrical conductivity. NBS publications such as "Resistance-temperature relation and thermoelectric properties of uranium" by Dahl and Van Dusen (1947) and "Freezing points of cobalt and nickel" by Van Dusen and Dahl (1947) fall into this category.  

Crucially, NBS was at the forefront of **instrumentation development for precision measurement**. Advances in measurement technology are often prerequisites for detecting subtle quantum effects or identifying anomalies. The 1948 paper by Roberts and Curtis, "An instrument for the rapid production of a decimal series of potentials and its application to ballistic measurements" , exemplifies this strand of research.  

The highly precise measurements of atomic spectra and fundamental constants emanating from NBS can serve as an essential **baseline for anomaly detection**. An "anomaly" in experimental data reported by another laboratory could potentially be identified if it significantly and inexplicably deviates from the established, precise values published by NBS around the same historical moment, assuming comparable experimental conditions and thorough uncertainty analysis. Thus, NBS data is not only a source of primary experimental results but also acts as a reference standard against which data from Bell Labs, MIT, and Princeton can be evaluated for the specific purpose of identifying unexplained variations or shifts in precision.

## III. Investigation of Measurement Anomalies and Precision Changes (Circa May 1948)

The period around May 1948 was one of extraordinary ferment in physics, with several groundbreaking discoveries and theoretical advancements reshaping the understanding of the quantum world. This context is crucial for interpreting any experimental data from that specific timeframe.

### A. Context: Major Physics Developments (Lamb Shift, QED, Transistor, Information Theory).

The late 1940s were a watershed for **Quantum Electrodynamics (QED)**. Experimental results, most notably the Lamb Shift, provided critical impetus and empirical grounding for the theoretical work of physicists like Richard Feynman, Julian Schwinger, Sin-Itiro Tomonaga, and Freeman Dyson. This era was characterized by a pressing need for high-precision experimental data to test and refine the nascent QED framework.  

The **Lamb Shift**, experimentally demonstrated by Willis Lamb and Robert Retherford at Columbia University in 1947, was a landmark discovery. Their experiments showed that the 2S1/2 and 2P1/2 energy levels in the hydrogen atom were not degenerate, a subtle difference not predicted by the existing Dirac theory of the electron. This was a profound "anomaly" that QED successfully explained. The initial paper announcing this finding was published in the  

_Physical Review_ in August 1947. By May 1948, this startling result would have been a major topic of discussion and a catalyst for precision atomic spectroscopy experiments at other leading institutions. Hans Bethe's influential non-relativistic calculation of the Lamb shift, also published in 1947 , further galvanized both theoretical and experimental efforts in this area.  

Contemporaneously, at Bell Labs, the **transistor** was invented, with key experiments occurring in December 1947 and the public announcement following in June 1948. This invention heralded the age of solid-state electronics, and the underlying semiconductor physics is fundamentally quantum mechanical. Experimental activities around May 1948 at Bell Labs would have been intensely focused on understanding, characterizing, and improving this new "transistor effect."  

Also at Bell Labs, Claude Shannon published his seminal papers "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" in the _Bell System Technical Journal_ in July and October 1948. While not directly experimental quantum physics, Shannon's revolutionary work on information theory provided new conceptual tools for understanding signal processing, noise, and the fundamental limits of measurement, which could have indirectly influenced the analysis and interpretation of experimental data across various scientific fields.  

The field of **microwave spectroscopy** was also gaining significant momentum. Charles Townes, who had worked at Bell Labs during the war, moved to Columbia University in 1948 and commenced his highly influential research program in this area. Microwave spectroscopy allows for extremely precise measurements of molecular and atomic quantum states, and its development was a direct application of wartime radar technology to fundamental science.  

This confluence of major experimental breakthroughs (Lamb Shift, transistor effect) that directly challenged existing theories and highlighted the urgent need for more precise measurements and new quantum mechanical explanations created a "pressure cooker" environment in the 1947-1948 period. Laboratories worldwide, particularly those with strong physics programs like Bell Labs, MIT, Princeton, and NBS, would have been eager to verify these new findings, explore their implications, or discover related phenomena. This heightened activity and intense focus on the frontiers of quantum physics make the period around May 1948 particularly fertile for the potential observation and reporting of further anomalies or rapid advancements in measurement precision.

### C. Preliminary Assessment of Potential Anomalies or Significant Precision Shifts.

Direct, explicit evidence of a _specific, widely recognized quantum physics anomaly occurring precisely in May 1948_ is not immediately apparent from the reviewed general historical summaries. The major, paradigm-shifting "anomalies" of that immediate post-war period, namely the Lamb Shift and the initial observations of the transistor effect, had their crucial experimental breakthroughs in 1947.

However, May 1948 falls squarely within a period of intense scientific activity spurred by these discoveries:
