# Collective Behavioral Patterns

Human behavior, both individual and collective, often shows telling shifts before and after catastrophic events. **Before** an event, we may search for subtle precursors or **psychological markers** hinting at the coming turmoil. In practice, truly predictive signals are elusive, but analysts can look at **social media sentiment, search query trends, and atypical crowd behaviors** for anomalies in the hours or days leading up to a disaster. For instance, one might analyze Twitter or Facebook posts for rising anxiety, aggression, or rumor-sharing about threats. In some cases, perpetrators themselves leave clues (as seen with some school shooters posting foreshadowing messages online), but those are individual signals. The question here is whether the **collective public** ever shows patterns that presage an event. Anecdotally, there have been reports of **unease or "pre-sentiment"** - people changing plans, avoiding certain places, or other unexplained decisions - prior to tragedies, but such cases tend to be subjective and hard to quantify. A more concrete approach is examining **Google Trends** data for unusual spikes in relevant queries (for example, searches about disaster preparedness, specific locations, or related conspiracy theories) in the 24-72 hours before an event. If a statistically significant surge in certain search terms occurred consistently before multiple incidents, it might indicate a genuine anomaly. However, so far there is **no consistent evidence of widespread collective precognition** via internet activity. Any apparent pre-event blips could be coincidental or related to smaller precursor news (e.g. people searching about a minor incident that unfortunately turns into a major one). Thus, in our analysis, we will scan for **pre-event sentiment or interest spikes** - say, a sudden jump in negative sentiment online or unusual traffic to certain keywords - but treat them cautiously.

In the **aftermath** of catastrophic events, collective behavior changes are pronounced and easier to document. In a real crisis, we see patterns like **911 lines jammed**, **hospitals flooded with victims**, **mass evacuations or migrations**, and often **panic buying after the event**. None of those occurred on Jan 1, 2000 - instead, all the "panic buying" was _before_ the date. For instance, in the COVID-19 pandemic or after natural disasters, public fear translates into concrete surges (runs on grocery stores, overwhelmed ERs, etc.).
